• Give Choices- Find ways for the child to gain an appropriate sense of control. Give lots of little choices as much as possible when things are going well.

Phrases for giving choices:

    • "Would you rather . . ."
    • "What would be best for you . . ."
    • "Feel free to . . ."
    • "What would help you the most . . ."
    • "Do you plan to . . ."
    • "Are you going to . . ."
  • Transfer the ownership of learning to student. Ask, "what will you do?" This question communicates volumes. First, that it is not the teacher's job to rescue a student. Second, the message being relayed is a confidence that the student has the capacity to solve their own problems.
  • Deliver the consequence with a strong dose of empathy.
    • "I can't imagine how bad you must feel. I had to give you a zero because I never got your work. Please let me know how I can help. I believe in you."
  • Focus on successes and gifts. Consistently catch your student doing good work and make a big deal out of it. Help identify what your student does well and highlight that strength as much as possible. It is not a way to give praise, but to describe what your student did correctly.

Examples:

    • "I notice that you always have your materials with you."
    • "I can read your print so well. How did you learn to print so neatly?"
