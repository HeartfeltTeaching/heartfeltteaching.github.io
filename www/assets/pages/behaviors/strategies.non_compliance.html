Accountability

Hold kids accountable to build responsibility and self-concept. Ask them questions to generate thinking. This sends a powerful message that their noncompliance is not the teacher's problem, and that there is a consequence the student will eventually face. Asking them questions prompts them to think through their problems and reflect on their behavior. Questions include:

  • What will you do about this problem?
  • What would you like to see happen?
  • How do you suppose this will work out?
  • On a scale of 1-5, how good of a decision do you think this is?
  • What do you think I think about that?
  • Would you like to hear what other kids have tried?
  • Some kids do A, some B. What do you think will work best for you?

Enforceable Statements

Setting and enforcing reasonable limits without raising your voice helps all to enjoy a predictable and consistent learning environment. Use enforceable statements by describing what you are willing to do or allow. Don't tell kids how to run their lives. Instead tell them how you are going to run yours.

  • "I will be glad to listen when your voice sounds as calm as mine."
  • "I will start when everyone is seated."
  • "I listen to students who raise their hands."
  • "I will discuss this when I feel respect is shown."
  • "I allow students to work in groups as long as the noise level remains soft."

Delay Consequences

Delaying the consequences gives you time to calm down, clear your head, and consult with others as needed. Not only does this buy you time, it will also signal that you are still in control.

  • "Oh no. This is sad. I'm going to have to do something about this. But not now, later. Try not to worry about it."
  • "I'm going to have to address this. We'll talk later."

Avoid a Power Struggle

Adopt a "consultative" type of leadership in the classroom. Practice providing students with multiple opportunities to make small choices. Instead of telling children what to do, encourage them to make decisions for themselves. Providing choices, not only helps children think for themselves, in many cases it may also motivate them. Be sure to give choices BEFORE resistance and not after.

  • "Would you rather do the even problems or the odd?"
  • "What would be best for you, math work or silent work first?"
  • "What would help you the most, to work with a partner or independently?
