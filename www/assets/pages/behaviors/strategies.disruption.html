Provide choices with limits primarily as a preventative measure. It will be difficult to provide effective choices in the face of a disruption. During these times, our brains tend to go into fight or flight mode, and we can't think of the best choices. As such, the only choices that come to mind are threats. "Settle down or go to the principal's office!" Thinking of choices usually requires a calm mind, and usually happens when you are experiencing a high level of cooperation. That is the time to offer two options that are equally acceptable.

Provide choices before resistance happens. Offering choices work better instead of giving a direct instruction to kids who have a history of noncompliance. Instead of saying, "Work with Joe on this project." Ask your student, "Would you rather work with Zach or Zoey on this project?"  Make sure that either option will be acceptable to you. Do not offer a choice that will not make you happy. If the student does not choose within a few seconds, choose for them.For example:

  • "Would you rather…"
  • "What would be best for you…"
  • "Feel free to…"
  • "What would help you the most…"

Recovery time as a strategy- Sometimes a disruptive student simply needs to be someplace else… temporarily, so the rest of the class has an opportunity to learn and the disrupting student has an opportunity to become calm.
