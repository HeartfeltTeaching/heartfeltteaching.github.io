Choices in the Classroom Setting

"You can play with the doll together, or I can figure out another toy for you to play with."

"You may turn in the assignment at the end of class or at the end of the day."

"Would you like to read the book together or with the audiobooks?"

"Would you like to present your project on a poster or computer?"

"You may work with a partner or choose to work independently."

"You may work at your desk or on the beanbag."

"You may choose to type or write out your responses."

"You may choose to do the odd problems first or the work even."

"You may choose a partner or have the teacher pick one for you."

"Your book report can be presented either orally or written."

 Sample Empathetic Statements in the Classroom Setting

"If I were in your position, I would feel the same way."

"That would frustrate me, too."

"I would be asking the same questions as you are."

"That's never good."

"That is so sad."

"Dang" or "What a drag."

"Ah man" or "Oh sweetie"

"Oh… this must be upsetting…"

 Examples of redirecting student behavior while maintaining the flow of instruction
  • Send a nonverbal message to student by displaying a hand gesture that indicates, "Please stop."
  • Move towards the student, while continuing to teach.
  • Ask the student to be a helper and send them to the office with an envelope, have them sharpen pencils, organize homework papers, or erase the board.
  • Ask the student a question about the lesson as you are teaching.
  • Quietly ask the student, "How can I help?"
  • Ask the student to move to another location, emphasizing that this is intended to help them avoid getting into trouble.
  • Whisper with empathy, "Oh...man…it looks like you’re having a hard day. If you want to talk about it, let’s set a time."
