Love and Logic (LL) is a positive behavioral research-based approach that was first established through the publications of Parenting with Love and Logic (Cine & Fay, 1990), and Parenting Teens with Love and Logic(Cline & Fay, 1992), and Teaching with Love and Logic(Fay & Funk, 1995). LL developers, Foster W. Cline and Charles Fay, wanted to focus on merging theory and practice and developed a set of useful and effective tools for helping support the emotional and behavioral well-being of children and thereby reducing educator stress levels.

LL integrates various theoretical core principles (e.g., Behavioral Theory, Resilience Theories, Social Learning Theory, and Cognitive and Attribution Theories). Those principles are centered on shared dignity and respect, shared thinking and problems solving, shared control within limits, sincere empathy, and positive adult-child relationships. This framework is designed to assist with effective decision making and teach consequences. Instead of relying on external motivators to influence behaviors, this behavior approach is based on the understanding that behavior is governed by a positive relationship with a significant adult.

Principle One: Maintain Mutual Dignity and Respect - Educators must demonstrate respect toward students while maintaining disciplinary interventions

Principle Two: Sincere Empathy- Educators communicate a strong dose of genuine understanding and compassion prior to expecting students to solve the problems they create.

Principle Three: Shared Thinking- Educators guide their students to solve the problems they create individually

Principle Four: Shared Control Within Limits- Educators allow their students to decide the smaller things that do not affect the welfare of others in the classroom.

Principle Five:Healthy Relationships- Intervention is focused on building and maintaining a relationship with students
